Part 1 — Brainstorming a Better Game-Creation Prompt

Ken

I experimented with this prompt — "I'm curious how creative you can be. Please create a NOVEL AI-powered web game." — with instances of Claude, Gemini, and ChatGPT. I was pleased with the results but then discovered the games were not as novel/original as I thought. I'm wondering if we can brainstorm a better prompt that would have a better chance of encouraging LLMs to come up with unique/novel/original/creative games.

Claude (author)
Thinking: Diagnosed LLM convergence patterns and synthesized six novelty-prompting strategies

This is a fascinating prompt engineering challenge — and it cuts right to a core limitation of LLMs. When you say "novel," the model thinks it's being novel, but it's really recombining the most statistically likely game patterns from its training data. You end up with variations on quiz games, word guessers, memory matches, etc.

Here are strategies worth exploring:

  1. Explicit anti-pattern listing — Tell the model what not to make: "Do not create any game that resembles: trivia/quiz games, word guessing, memory matching, snake, breakout, platformers, tower defense, or any game mechanic you can find described on Wikipedia's list of game genres."
  2. Two-phase prompting — ideate then build — "First, brainstorm 10 game concepts that have never existed before. For each, explain what makes it genuinely novel. Then pick the best and build it."
  3. Combinatorial constraint injection — Give it two or three unrelated seeds to collide: "Create a game that combines the mechanics of [weather systems], [social reputation], and [sound]."
  4. Ground it in a unique AI capability — "Create a game where the AI dynamically generates the rules based on how the player behaves" or "…where the AI acts as an unreliable narrator whose story the player must fact-check."
  5. The meta-cognitive frame — "You are about to create a web game. Before you do, reflect on what games LLMs typically produce when asked for 'a novel game' and why they are predictable. Then create something that avoids every pattern you just described."
Ken

I will experiment with your prompt but I wonder if we could enhance it to ask the LLM to do "Combinatorial constraint injection" but to self-generate the random elements rather than me providing them.

Claude (author)
Thinking: Recognized self-generating constraints as clever enhancement strategy

Great idea — make the model do its own creative collision rather than us hand-picking the seeds. Here's the enhanced prompt:

"I want you to create a truly original AI-powered web game — something that has genuinely never been made before. Follow this process:

Step 1: List 5 game concepts that LLMs would typically generate for this request, and explain why they are predictable.
Step 2: Randomly select 3 unrelated domains from different areas of human experience (e.g., biology, architecture, maritime law). Choose domains that seem genuinely incompatible — the more absurd the combination, the better.
Step 3: Brainstorm 8 game concepts that emerge from unexpected collisions between your chosen domains.
Step 4: Critically evaluate each concept for genuine originality — would a player say 'I've never seen anything like this before'?
Step 5: Build it."

One thing to watch for: models may "cheat" by picking domains that conveniently converge on something familiar.

Part 2 — Claude Runs the Prompt & Creates Substrate v1

Claude (author)
Thinking: Architected novel game merging mycology, diplomacy, and tidal mechanics

Step 1 — Five predictable games LLMs typically generate

  • Trivia/Quiz — AI generates questions, player answers. The lowest-friction use of a language model's knowledge.
  • Word Guessing/Wordle clone — AI picks a word, gives clues. Word games map directly to what LLMs do.
  • Text Adventure/Interactive Fiction — AI narrates, player chooses. It's literally chatbot conversation with a fantasy skin.
  • AI Dungeon-style RPG — AI as game master. The canonical "AI game" that every model has seen.
  • Drawing/Description game — AI describes, player guesses (or vice versa). Showcases generation as a parlor trick.

All five use AI as a content generator sitting outside the game. The AI could be replaced by a database and the core mechanic wouldn't change.

Step 2 — Three random unrelated domains

  • Mycology (fungal networks) — Fungi communicate through mycelial networks, share resources, can be parasitic or symbiotic.
  • Diplomatic protocol — The rigid, coded world of formal diplomacy where micro-gestures carry enormous meaning.
  • Tidal systems — Water moves in response to gravitational forces, creating rhythms of concealment and revelation.

Why these: they seem genuinely incompatible. One is underground and slow, one is surface-level and performative, one is cyclical and indifferent.

Step 3–4 — Concepts evaluated, winner selected

After brainstorming 8 collision concepts, Concept 8 — "Mycelial Intelligence" emerged as the most genuinely novel: you operate entirely in the hidden layer, never seeing the diplomatic surface. The AI generates cryptic chemical dispatches — your only intelligence. You manipulate a mycelial network to influence diplomatic relationships between organisms, timed to tidal pulses.

Step 5 — The game: Substrate

A dark, bioluminescent web game where you play as a hidden mycelial network. Six fungal organisms (Cordyceps, Tremella, Amanita, Mycena, Russula, Pholiota) arranged in a circle have hidden diplomatic relationships (-3 hostile to +3 bonded). Your tools:

  • Probe — Click an organism; the AI generates a cryptic chemical dispatch about its diplomatic state.
  • Connect — Click two organisms to weave a mycelial thread. When the tide pulses, threads transmit influence, improving relationships.
  • The Tide — Sweeps through every 14 seconds, amplifying influence but also decaying some bonds. 7 cycles total.

Goal: Forge three target alliances (relationship ≥ warm) before the final tide.

The AI is your unreliable sensory organ — the only perceptual interface between you and a world you cannot directly observe.

Part 3 — Claude Reviewer Plays Substrate v1

A second Claude instance (Sonnet 4.6, operating with computer-use tools) was asked to play the game and provide feedback in the original chat.
Claude (reviewer)

I played through several rounds of Substrate v1 and found both genuine strengths and significant bugs.

What works beautifully

  • The bioluminescent aesthetic is stunning — dark backgrounds with glowing orbs create an atmospheric, eerie feel.
  • The concept is genuinely novel — playing as a hidden mycelial intelligence with an AI as your "unreliable sensory organ" is unlike anything I've seen.
  • The intro screen explaining the mechanics is well-written.

Bugs and UX issues

  1. Connection mechanic is broken or very unintuitive — The instructions say "click two to connect" but clicking an organism always triggers a probe (costing energy). The selected state seems to clear before you can click a second organism due to a React async state timing issue. I could only connect organisms once by accident in multiple attempts.
  2. Page scroll conflict — When a dispatch generates, it scrolls the parent page down, hiding the game canvas. Players must manually scroll back up constantly, completely breaking the flow.
  3. Tide cycle is too fast — 14 seconds per tide × 7 tides = 98 seconds total. AI dispatch generation alone takes 5–10 seconds. Just reading one dispatch consumes ~10–15% of total time. Nearly impossible to forge 3 alliances.
  4. No visual feedback for mycelial threads — Even when a connection was created, it was hard to tell the thread was actually there.

Suggestion: The connection mechanic needs a clear "first click selects, second click connects" flow with obvious visual highlighting. Also consider pausing tides while a dispatch is loading.

Part 4 — Claude Author Fixes All Four Issues → Substrate v2

Claude (author)
Thinking: Identified four critical bugs and strategized comprehensive fixes

Excellent feedback — these are real usability problems. The core issue is that probe and connect are overloaded onto the same click gesture, which makes connecting nearly impossible. Let me fix all four issues.

Substrate v2 — Four fixes
  1. Separate Probe/Connect modes — Explicit toggle bar at the top. In Probe mode, clicking an organism requests a dispatch. In Connect mode, clicking one organism selects it (with an animated pulsing ring), then clicking another connects them. Free — no energy cost.
  2. Scroll fixed — Dispatches scroll only within their own panel. Game view has height: 100vh; overflow: hidden. No more page hijacking.
  3. Tides slowed + paused during AI — Interval extended from 14s to 25s. Tide pauses entirely while dispatch loads, showing a "⏸ PAUSED" indicator.
  4. Much more visible threads — Multiple layered strokes: thick glow, solid core, animated particle flow, pulsing endpoint dots.

Part 6 — Relationship-Responsive Threads → Substrate v3

Claude (author)
Thinking: Architected visual feedback system for relationship progression tracking

Good catch — without feedback on whether your threads are actually working, you're playing blind in a game that's already about limited visibility. The thread itself should be your feedback channel, which is thematically perfect: the mycelium would pulse differently as the relationship it's carrying changes.

Substrate v3 — Visual feedback layers on each thread
  • Color shifts from red (hostile) through teal (neutral) to green (warm/allied)
  • Width and glow intensity grow as the relationship improves
  • Particle flow speed increases with stronger relationships
  • Text label at midpoint shows current state: "toxic", "wary", "neutral", "warm", "allied", "bonded"
  • Score pips — 7 tiny dots beneath the label showing position on -3 to +3 scale, colored red → grey → green

This means you can now connect early and watch the thread evolve through each tidal pulse — which turns the waiting between tides from dead time into tension.

Part 9 — Ken Asks About the Prompts Inside the Game

Ken

describe the prompts used in this game

Claude (author)
Thinking: Identified request to explain game prompt mechanics

There are two prompts in the API call — a system prompt that establishes the AI's role, and a user prompt that feeds it the specific game state.

System prompt:

You are the chemical-signal interpreter of a mycelial fungal network. You produce dispatches about an organism's diplomatic relationships with its neighbors. RULES: Never state numbers or scores directly. Use biological metaphor — nutrient flows, toxin gradients, substrate chemistry, hyphal behavior. Include directional hints (eastward, northern neighbor, etc.) that correspond to the organism's position in a circular arrangement. Keep dispatches to 2–3 evocative sentences.

User prompt (constructed from live game state):

Generate a chemical dispatch for [ORGANISM NAME] at position [N] in a circle of 6. Its current relationships:
[Neighbor] (direction): relationship score [X] (range -3 hostile to +3 bonded)
[Neighbor] (direction): relationship score [Y] (range -3 hostile to +3 bonded)
…

So the model receives the complete ground truth — every neighbor's name, direction, and exact numeric score — and its job is to encode that into metaphor. The player's job is to decode it back. The AI sits between the game state and the player as a poetic translation layer.

What makes this work as a game mechanic is the asymmetry: the model knows everything and must obscure it artfully, while the player knows nothing and must interpret. The directional hints ("eastward colony") are the key bridging mechanism — they give you enough to map dispatch language onto the spatial layout without the AI just telling you "Russula and Amanita are allies."

It's worth noting that the 2–3 sentence constraint is doing important game design work. If the dispatches were one sentence, they'd be too cryptic. If they were a full paragraph, they'd give away too much and reduce the interpretive challenge. The prompt is essentially tuning the difficulty curve.
