Exploring the 1918 Influenza Simulator

This guide helps you use the simulation like a historian and a model-builder: look for patterns, ask what evidence supports them, and test how different choices might change the spread of disease.

What You Are Looking At

The Map

The dots are important places in the model. The lines show simplified ship, rail, troop, and labor routes.

The Controls

You can change the origin hypothesis, war setting, public-health timing, and policies such as quarantine or school closures.

The Graph

The graph shows historical-scale estimates for infected, recovered, deaths, and deaths per month. It grows as the simulation plays.

Start Here

Run the historical setting once

Press play and watch the map and graph from January 1918 to June 1920. Notice when the biggest changes happen.

Click for evidence

Click a city marker, country, route, graph, or information button. The right panel explains what that item represents and links to sources where possible.

Compare origin hypotheses

Try Kansas / Camp Funston, Étaples / Western Front, and northern China / labor routes. Ask: what changes early, and what stays similar later?

Test a counterfactual

Change only one setting at a time. For example, compare the historical war setting with no world war, or switch on port quarantine.

Use Ask carefully

Ask questions such as “Why was the autumn wave worse?” or “Compare Australia and New Zealand deaths per person.” If web search is on, check the sources it cites.

Good Questions To Try

  • Which places become active early, and which become active later?
  • How do ship and rail routes change the timing of outbreaks?
  • What happens if port quarantine starts earlier?
  • Does “no world war” remove the pandemic, or mostly change how it spreads?
  • Why does the graph show several waves instead of one smooth curve?
  • Which claims are backed by historical data, and which are model assumptions?
  • How would you improve the model if you had more data?

Mini Investigations

Investigation A: Waves

Turn on only “Deaths per month.” Play the simulation. Record which wave is highest and when it happens.

Investigation B: Movement

Click ship and rail routes. Explain why transport systems matter for an airborne disease.

Investigation C: Policy

Compare late and early public-health timing. Write one sentence about what changes and one sentence about what does not.

How To Read The Model

A simulation is not the same thing as the past itself. It is a simplified way of testing ideas about the past. This simulator uses historical estimates and real map data, but some route weights and counterfactual settings are teaching assumptions.

Useful phrase: “The model suggests…” is usually better than “This proves…”.

Exit Ticket

Before you finish, answer these in a few sentences:

  1. What is one pattern you noticed in the map?
  2. What is one pattern you noticed in the graph?
  3. What is one historical claim you would want to check with more evidence?
  4. What counterfactual setting changed the outcome most in your exploration?
