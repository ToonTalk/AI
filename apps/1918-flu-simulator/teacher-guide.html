Teaching With the 1918 Influenza Simulator

This guide is for planning lessons around the simulator. It emphasizes historical reasoning, model literacy, data uncertainty, and thoughtful use of AI-assisted question answering.

Learning Goals

Historical Reasoning

Students distinguish evidence, uncertainty, and interpretation in competing accounts of pandemic origins and spread.

Systems Thinking

Students connect disease transmission to transport networks, war, crowding, public-health choices, and timing.

Model Literacy

Students learn that simulations simplify reality and that counterfactuals are tools for inquiry, not proof.

Before Class

Technical Setup

  • Open index.html in a browser.
  • No server is required for the simulation itself.
  • The Ask panel requires an OpenAI API key if you use it.
  • For student use, consider demonstrating Ask from the teacher device rather than sharing a key.
Important: This is a historically grounded classroom model, not a complete reconstruction. The country map is public-domain Natural Earth data, but transport route weights and many counterfactual settings are teaching assumptions.

Suggested 45-60 Minute Lesson

5 min

Introduce the question: why did the 1918 influenza pandemic spread globally, and why did timing matter?

10 min

Run the default historical setting as a class. Ask students to watch for transport routes and wave timing.

10 min

Have pairs choose one origin hypothesis and one counterfactual setting. They should change only one variable at a time.

10 min

Students collect two observations: one from the map and one from the graph. They must also identify one uncertainty.

10 min

Discuss: what did the model make visible, and what did it hide?

5 min

Exit ticket: one evidence-backed claim, one model limitation, and one question for further research.

Activities

Origin Hypothesis Comparison

Groups compare Kansas, Étaples, and northern China. Focus on what changes early and what converges later.

Public Health Timing

Students compare late, mixed, and early public-health timing with the same origin and war setting.

Transport Network Inquiry

Students click routes and explain why maritime and rail systems made an airborne disease a global event.

Waves and Graph Reading

Turn on deaths per month. Students identify the first, second, third, and regional recurrence periods.

Ask With Sources

Use the Ask panel to investigate a place not emphasized in the model. Students inspect whether the sources support the answer.

Improve the Model

Students propose one new dataset or variable that would make the simulation more historically accurate.

Discussion Prompts

  • What does the map make easier to understand than a paragraph would?
  • Why might a country with fewer total deaths still have a higher death rate per person?
  • How did World War I affect movement, crowding, reporting, and medical capacity?
  • What is the difference between “the model says” and “history shows”?
  • What sources would you trust for mortality estimates, and why?
  • How should we talk about uncertainty without making students think “anything goes”?

Assessment Ideas

Evidence of Learning What to Look For
Map explanation Student links spread to movement routes, not just distance.
Graph explanation Student identifies wave timing and distinguishes monthly deaths from total deaths.
Counterfactual claim Student changes one setting at a time and avoids overclaiming.
Source critique Student notices when data are approximate, incomplete, or model-derived.

Using the Ask Panel

The Ask panel can help students pursue questions that the model does not directly answer, especially when web search is enabled. It is best used as a source-aware tutor, not as an authority.

Good Uses

  • Ask for a comparison between two countries.
  • Ask why a historical source might disagree with another.
  • Ask for a plain-language explanation of a model term.
  • Ask for a small graph using cited data.

Teacher Cautions

  • Do not expose a private API key to students.
  • Ask students to check cited sources.
  • Remind students that AI answers can mix model outputs and historical data unless prompted carefully.
  • Use follow-up questions to ask “What evidence supports that?”

Known Limitations

  • The model uses selected places rather than every country and region.
  • Transport weights are simplified and are not exact passenger, troop, or freight counts.
  • Some national mortality estimates are uncertain or contested.
  • Counterfactual settings show plausible mechanisms, not verified alternate histories.
  • Public-health controls are simplified into timing and strength rather than local legal and social details.

Extension Ideas

  • Have students build a small country comparison table using cited sources.
  • Ask students to design one new route or node and justify it historically.
  • Compare 1918 public-health interventions with a later pandemic response.
  • Have students write a short critique titled “What this model hides.”
