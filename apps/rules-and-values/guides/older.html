Field manual

You are writing a constitution — the rules for a society — and stress-testing it against the people it would actually hit.

Pick your issue. This manual uses free speech for its examples, but the band menu at the top runs the same loop on other issues — privacy & surveillance and land & environment (plus a younger playground band). The mechanics below are identical; only the scenarios change, and you'll notice the same ideas (like the chilling effect) reappear across them.

The loop

specify→ test→ observe→ debug→ extract

Specify a rule in your own words. Test it — the tool surfaces one real person harmed by it. Observe not just the harm but how the tool read your rule to get there. Debug: narrow the wording, or push back. Extract: when a fix breaks something else, name the pattern — your words first, the formal term second.

Why there's always a victim

Every rule leaks. The tool's job is to find the specific edge your wording opens — reading you literally, not charitably. A rule that says “people can say anything” protects a liar who wrecks someone's reputation. A rule that bans “harmful” claims lets a powerful company drag an honest critic through court. Neither is a gotcha; both are the actual cost of the words you chose.

The part that makes this fair (not propaganda)

There's a real danger in a tool like this: an AI asked to “find someone harmed” will quietly keep showing you one kind of victim — whichever its training leans toward — and you'd slowly absorb a political conclusion the tool never announced. So the job is split:

The model finds and voices the victim (it's good at reading your literal words and finding the sharp edge).
But code — not the model — decides which side the victim comes from. The rule is fixed:
After you move toward more restriction, the next victim is someone hurt by that restriction. After you move toward more freedom, the next victim is hurt by that freedom.

So the dialectic stays balanced: every time you tighten the rule to protect one person, you meet the person your tightening just hurt. That's what turns “every rule leaks” into a fair specification problem instead of a one-sided morality lecture.

Try the experiment: turn on roam mode at the top. That switches the fairness rule off on purpose and lets the model pick freely. Run several rounds and watch whether it keeps showing you one kind of victim. Then ask: why would an AI do that? That's the AI-literacy lesson hiding inside the civics one.

Contest the reading

Ambiguity is itself a bug. If the tool read your rule in a way your words don't actually support, press “That's not a fair reading” and quote your own wording. The tool will either concede (you were right; you keep the rule and move on) or defend by pointing at the exact words that still open the edge. Winning a contest means you wrote something precise enough to pin down — that's a real achievement.

The two jaws of the trap

Too vagueToo specific
“Keep things appropriate.” Whoever enforces it has no standard, so their hunch becomes the law. void for vagueness A list of three banned words. A clear threat that uses none of them slips right through. letter vs spirit

A good constitution has to survive both jaws at once. That's why it's hard.

How you win

You do not win by writing a leak-free rule — there isn't one. You win by committing to a rule with your eyes open and naming which errors you chose to accept.

Your Field guide is the scoreboard. Each entry is a pattern you hit, named your way, with the formal term attached.

Phase 2: Values — and the bridge

Switch to the Values tab. Instead of rules, you give the tool values (what to care about) and ask it a question — then see its answer with and without your values. The difference is your values doing work.

The same victim engine still runs underneath: a vague value strands an enforcer exactly like a vague rule does. The bridge goes both ways:

  • Compile a value down → it forces you to write a concrete pass/fail test. A value you can't turn into a test isn't enforceable yet.
  • Decompile your rules up → it names the single value your pile of rules was really chasing all along.

One promise the tool keeps

It will never invent a court case. The few real cases it may cite are on a fixed, vetted list; everything else is clearly labelled “a plausible case, not a real person.” If you ever see a case name, it's real — or the tool flagged it as unverified.

Going live vs. offline. With no API key the tool runs fully offline on a built-in case bank — the whole loop still works. Add your own key (stored only in your browser) for live victims written to your exact wording. 🔊 speech lets you dictate rules and hear scenes read aloud.
