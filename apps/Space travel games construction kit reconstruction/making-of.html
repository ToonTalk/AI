The Making of the Metagame

How a 20-year-old Imagine Logo program — a game about making games — was excavated from its own source files and rebuilt to run in a modern browser, one conversation at a time.

What this is

"A Space Travel Games Construction Kit" is a metagame: a game whose goal is to make games. The player is hired by a studio, given a team of experts, and builds space games by collecting behaviours and attaching them to objects. Along the way they absorb real physics, mathematics, history, art, and programming — never as a lecture, always because the next game needs it. It was created by Ken about twenty years ago in Imagine Logo, and ran as a browser plugin that the modern web no longer supports.

The starting point

We began with a folder of originals: the Imagine Logo .IMP project files (lunar589.IMP, lunar402b.IMP, diana.IMP), the original media (astronaut and lander sprites, starfield and moonscape backgrounds, hand-drawn team portraits, a Phut.wav sound), the old plugin launch pages, two Flash demo wrappers, a paper Ken wrote about the system, and two screen recordings Ken made of the original running. There was also an earlier reconstruction that didn't follow the original very faithfully.

The key decision: rather than invent new text, we treated the .IMP files as the source of truth. Hidden inside them — as readable strings and RTF — were the exact narrative panels, jokes, the conservation-of-momentum explanation, every gadget name, the four "reached ship" code boxes, the help messages, and the physics constants. The rebuild mines those originals.

What the originals told us

The recovered text set the whole tone. The hire speech ("Congratulations! We've decided to hire you..."), Tist's momentum lesson, Ian's "trillion trillion molecules every second," the ice-skater- with-a-fire-extinguisher analogy, Signer's four conditional actions, and the real numbers — an astronaut massing 95 kg without rocks, rocks thrown at 100 m/s, a 12 m/s safe docking speed, 120 seconds of air, moon gravity of 1.6 m/s². And the gadgets' actual Logo programs, like:

; [HORIZONTAL ROCK THROWING CODE]
change_my_horizontal_velocity_by
    -1 * value_of_slider_for_horizontal_rock_mass
        * value_of_slider_for_horizontal_rock_speed
        / (value_of_slider_for_mass_without_rocks
           + my_horizontal_rocks_remaining)

How it was built, conversation by conversation

  1. A faithful first pass. The original's look (black starfield, cyan gadget boxes with SETTINGS / HELP / FLIP, yellow story notes, the classic plugin chrome), the hire-you-then- meet-the-team intro, the Rescue gadgets, and conservation-of-momentum physics.
  2. Make it a real construction kit. The Director (CEO) became the narrator and Signer the assistant designer; behaviours are no longer free — you collect them by visiting the team. Gadgets attach to the back of objects by drag-and-drop, and you're allowed to drop one on the wrong object and see it fail.
  3. The teaching loop. You build an incomplete prototype, test it (no rocks are thrown until you throw them), watch the astronaut slide uselessly past the ship, and only then — after you press Stop — does the kit point you back to the team, where Signer proposes the ending and Jammer programs it.
  4. Gated dialogue with memory. Each teammate only reveals new material when you've earned it (Jammer won't hand over gadgets until Tist has explained the physics), uses "That's all for now" when they're tapped out, and remembers how far you've gotten so revisits jump ahead instead of repeating.
  5. A second game. The Lunar Lander — gravity, a throttle-driven thruster, a landing action — collected the same way. Plus the pedagogical centrepiece: record a flight, turn it into editable Logo schedule code, and run it back on auto-pilot.
  6. Polish and honesty. Graphical dial gauges (including thrust), draggable starting positions, a 2-D rescue world, a visible oxygen clock, formatted win/lose messages — and a retuned lander, because testing proved the original numbers made a safe landing physically impossible. It is now winnable with fuel to spare.

Notes from under the hood

physics Everything moves by conservation of momentum. Throwing a rock of mass m at speed v changes the astronaut's velocity by -m·v / total mass; the lander's thruster is the same idea aimed downward. Gravity and the safe-speed checks come straight from the original's numbers.

record → code → replay A flight is captured as [time, throttle] pairs, written out as schedule 1.2 [set_throttle 80] lines you can edit, then replayed by a scheduler. Playing becomes programming — the deepest idea in the kit.

build The whole app is a single self-contained index.html (CSS and JavaScript inlined) so it runs anywhere and previews reliably. The editable sources live alongside it as data.js (all recovered text), app.js (logic), and styles.css.

verification With no browser available in the build sandbox, correctness was checked by loading the page in a headless DOM and driving it like a player — proving the acquisition flow, the win/lose conditions, a beatable rescue, and a safe landing whose recorded auto-pilot also lands.

Why a metagame?

The kit is built on a simple, radical idea: people learn the most when they are making something they care about. You don't study momentum and then apply it; you need momentum to get one astronaut home, so you learn it. You don't take a programming course; you flip a gadget over, read its little program, and change the numbers in red. History, art, and sound arrive as the people you consult, not as chapters. It is, in the best sense, hard fun — and twenty years on, it still works.
