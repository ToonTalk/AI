What is happening here?

Each specimen is a drawing made by a tiny program, shown below the picture. A turtle holds a coloured pen and follows the instructions — forward, turn, change colour, stamp a dot.

The whole program sits inside REPEAT n [ … ]. The turtle runs everything in the brackets n times over, each pass carrying on from where the last one stopped — so the line coils, loops, or spirals on itself. Change that one number and the whole shape grows or tightens.

When you pick a specimen you like, six children are bred from it. Each child carries the same program, but chance has made a few small changes — marked in red in each child's program. The changes are not chosen on purpose; they happen first, by accident, in every child. Then you choose which child becomes a parent. That is how people breed dogs, roses, and pigeons. (Later we will let nature do the choosing instead of you.)

And you needn't pick for beauty. Choose the ugliest one every time, or the simplest, the spikiest, the most lopsided — any rule you like, even a silly one. Whatever you consistently favour is the direction the lineage drifts. That choice of criterion is the whole of it: artificial selection is just a breeder applying some preference, over and over.

The Pattern menu at the bottom controls how much nested repetition new specimens may contain — from plain shapes to deeper, more intricate ones.

Switch Selection to The Wild and a habitat does the choosing instead of you. It scores every specimen by a simple, visible rule — a sunny meadow favours big, colourful blooms; a deep cave favours small, plain ones — and the better a specimen fits, the more children it tends to leave. Press Let a season pass to watch the population drift, generation by generation, toward whatever the habitat rewards. Nothing is aiming at that result: the variation is still random, and the habitat simply lets some reproduce more than others. Try the same start in two habitats and watch them diverge.

One habitat, A Naturalist's Eye, hands the judging to an A.I. (Claude, Gemini, or ChatGPT) that looks at each specimen and rates it against a niche you write in plain words. Inside Claude.ai it runs without a key; elsewhere you supply your own. It is open-ended and fun, but remember an A.I.'s taste is its own — it may not impose a clean or steady pressure.

You can also invent your own habitat with ＋ New habitat: slide each trait toward what it should reward. And the Ask a question button opens a friendly guide you can ask about evolution, about your own line of descent, or about how this field guide works and was made.

The Line of Descent keeps every ancestor you have chosen, so you can watch tiny changes pile up over generations. Tap an old ancestor to go back and breed down a different path. Click edit under any specimen to change its program by hand. Use Save… above the strip to keep the whole lineage as a web page — one expandable card per generation, with its habitat (and, for the A.I. judge, the niche) recorded.

This little world owes its idea to Richard Dawkins's biomorphs from The Blind Watchmaker (1986): a program in which you breed branching figures by repeatedly selecting among randomly mutated offspring, watching "cumulative selection" build up surprising forms from almost nothing. Specimens replaces his branching trees with turtle-graphics programs, but the lesson is the same. Read about The Blind Watchmaker and biomorphs.
